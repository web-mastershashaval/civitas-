UX DESIGN: WRITING RULES WITHOUT TURNING USERS INTO LAWYERS
Core UX Principle (Non-Negotiable)

Users do not write rules.
They configure behavior.

If you ask people to “write rules”, you lose 80% of them.

1. The Mental Model Users Can Handle

Instead of “Rules”, the UI presents:

Community Behavior Settings

Language matters.

People understand:

“What happens if someone does X?”
Not:

“Define enforcement policies”

2. Rule Creation as a Guided Flow (Not a Blank Page)
❌ What NOT to do

Blank text editor

Markdown rules

Free-form policy writing

✅ What WORKS

A wizard-style builder with guardrails.

3. The Rule Builder UX (Step-by-Step)
STEP 1: Choose the Problem (Intent-Based)

Instead of “Create Rule”, show:

“What do you want to control?”

Options (examples):

Harassment & abuse

Spam & promotions

Off-topic posts

Payments & monetization

Member behavior

Content quality

This reduces cognitive load.

STEP 2: Define the Trigger (Plain Language)

Example UI:

When this happens…

A message is reported

A new member posts

A link is shared

A payment is requested

A rule is violated again

No technical words.

STEP 3: Set Conditions (Sliders & Toggles)

Instead of logic, use controls:

Number of reports: 2 – 10

Time window: 10 min – 7 days

Member age: new / established

Role: any / contributor / mod

Behind the scenes → logic.

User sees → intent.

STEP 4: Choose Consequences (Pre-Defined Only)

No free-text punishments.

Selectable actions:

Warn user

Temporarily mute (choose duration)

Remove content

Require review

Trigger vote

Escalate to next level

This enforces no bending rules.

STEP 5: Preview the Rule (This Is Huge)

Before saving, show:

“If this happens, this will occur.”

Example:

“If 3 members report a message for harassment within 24 hours, the message will be removed and the author muted for 48 hours.”

This builds confidence and trust.

4. Rule Templates (Your Secret Weapon)

Most communities are not unique.

You should ship opinionated templates:

Professional learning group

Support group

Faith-based community

Developer community

Paid mentorship group

Templates:

reduce mistakes

encode best practices

speed onboarding

⚠️ Hard truth:
Templates shape culture.
This is where your values sneak in — intentionally.

5. Rule Complexity Is Layered (Not Dumped)
Beginner View

“Low / Medium / Strict”

Simple toggles

No advanced settings

Advanced View

Thresholds

Escalation paths

Voting triggers

Versioning

You hide power until it’s needed.

6. Make Rules Feel Fair, Not Punitive

UX copy matters.

Avoid:

“Punishment”

“Penalty”

“Strike”

Use:

“Warning”

“Cooling-off”

“Review”

“Protection”

Tone shapes community psychology.

7. Versioning UX (This Builds Trust)

When rules change:

Members see:

what changed

why

when it applies

Must re-accept before continuing

Old violations remain bound to old rules

No surprises.

8. Show Enforcement Visibly (Without Shaming)

Example UI elements:

“This message was removed under Rule R-003”

“3 messages muted today for spam”

“2 appeals reversed this week”

Transparency without drama.

9. The Biggest UX Risk (Be Honest)

⚠️ Over-configuration fatigue

If:

setup takes too long

too many decisions upfront

Users quit.

Solution:

Default safe templates

Progressive disclosure

“Edit later” allowed, but enforcement only after activation

10. Why This UX Is Defensible

Competitors struggle because:

they fear restricting admins

they fear complexity

they fear transparency

You embrace all three.

That’s your moat.

11. Reality Check (I Won’t Lie)

This UX will:

slow onboarding by ~10–20%

reduce impulsive community creation

But it will:

massively increase trust

attract serious communities

reduce moderation chaos

Quality over quantity.

12. Lock This Principle In (Final)

If a rule cannot be explained in one sentence, it cannot be enforced.
